Exploring Dementia Progression with Data Mining

We investigate patterns and correlations in dementia using a longitudinal dataset and data mining techniques to support early intervention and better management strategies.

Background

  • Definition: Dementia is a syndrome marked by cognitive decline beyond normal aging, affecting memory, reasoning, and daily function.
  • Global Impact: Over 55 million people are affected worldwide; the burden is social, emotional, and economic.
  • Research Challenge: Complex interplay of age, cognition, and lifestyle; limited awareness and stigma impede early diagnosis and care.
  • Project Focus: Use data mining to uncover patterns that can inform earlier detection and care planning.

Research Questions

  1. How do diseases and brain injuries influence dementia severity?
  2. What is the correlation between aging and dementia progression?
  3. How do patient activities and lifestyle affect dementia risk?

Methodology

Data Preparation

  • Imputation: MMSE → mean; SES → median
  • Encoding: Demented = 1, Non‑Demented = 2
  • Normalization for clustering

Main Algorithms

  • K‑Means clustering on Age & CDR (and MMSE)
  • Cluster interpretation vs. diagnosis

Key Findings

Correlations

Strong relationship between cognitive tests and severity: MMSE ↔ CDR = −0.687. Age shows weak correlation with severity (−0.025).

Cluster Patterns

  • Cluster 0: Younger (~71) with severe dementia
  • Cluster 1: Older (~82) with milder dementia

Coverage

Answered RQ#2 (age ↔ progression); partial evidence for RQ#3 (activities & demographics) via correlation review.

Technical Challenges

  • Limited feature set (Age, MMSE, CDR) constrained model accuracy.
  • Missing values in key variables required careful imputation.
  • Overlapping characteristics between mild and non‑demented groups complicated clustering.
  • Manual algorithm tuning for convergence and interpretability.
  • Clear visualization of clusters and correlations required multiple plot types.
